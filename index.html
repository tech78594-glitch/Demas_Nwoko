This is Baba's story.

His story of the langauge called Vernacular—the natural langauge of design—Baba's heartbeat and legacy

The Story

We remember our quick three-hour session with Baba in Ibadan.

We were researching Africanfuturism in Architecture and its impact on the African's health and wellness. At the time, there was not much work on this. Baba's was one of the authentic few.

For me, it felt like a dream—encountering in person someone we had only read about in our Visual Arts classes.

Among the many invaluable wisdoms Baba deposited in us during that session, one thing stood out:
Baba did not care for labels—African or traditional

He recounted receiveing an award abroad, where foreigners asked him what type of architecture he identified with.
Was it vernacular?

He replied:
"if it is not vernacular, what else is there? Vernacular is your mother's tongue—that is your nature. So how can yu design for your client without understanding his vernacular?"

To Baba, vernacular is the natural langauge of design.

So this mini-hybrid exhibit project takes on the life of Baba's vernacular—to visually reinterpret and explore his heartbeat through art.

As architects, we took to the paintbrush, the pen, and the mouse to tell Baba's story.
So, join us for the ride.

The Journey

We already know Baba's history. Check Google if you don't.
We are not here for that—rather, we are here to tell his story

We did not begin this work by asking who Demas Nwoko is.

we began by listening to what his work has been saying all along.

Across bukidings, artworks, spaces, and silences, one idea kept returning—quietly, insitently, without explanation:
Vernacular is the natural langauge of design.

This project is not merely an archive or a catalogue.
It is our journey of encountering that philosophy, sitting with it, and allowing it to shape how we see space, culture, and making.

It is our journey of capturing Baba's heart,
telling his story,and shaping his legacy.

His Roots His Future

Baba's journey into vernacular began before the days of the Zaria Art Society.

from the start, Baba sought not to imitate foreign aesthetics, but to root modern art and architecture in the soil of Nigerian life—its materials, rhythms, and cosmology.

He saw design not as a borrowed langauge, but as a living dialect of place.

In his words and in his practice, vernacular was not a limitation—it was the heartbeat of design itself.

It was how culture spoke through space, texture, and form.

His Roots His Future

So when Temi paints this piece, she tells the story of his beginning—
his longing to tell the story of his fathers.

And so we painted patterns that speak of material honesty, spatial rhythm, and continuity.

You see how firmly he held onto his roots to shape his future.

We dare say:
His roots are his future.

Vernacular, in Demas Nwoko's work, is not nostalgia.

It is not tradition preserved behind glass.

It is not the past repeated.

It is langauge—a living system through which people, materials, climate, sound, ritual, and belief spaek to one another.

A langauge learned not in classrooms, but through proximity—to land, to craft, to use, to meaning.

In his architecture, walls do not merely enclose space;
they respond—to heat, to light, to movement, to gathering.

In his art, form deos not decorate; it remembers.
In his philosophy, design is not imposed—it emerges.

To work vernacularly is not to reject modernity,
but to translate it.

His Heartbeat, His Legacy

And so, in trying to capture his heartbeat, Temi remembers the saying:
"The eyes are the window to the soul."

There was no better way than to trace each line with his words,
to lay each colour with his fire,
and to draw from the permanence of his heart

His Heartbeat, His Legacy

The worth of a man is not valued by how much he got,
but by how much he gave—
not from the valleys of his hands,
but from the depths of his heart.

That is his real legacy.

That is excatly what are captured in this piece—
his legacy not merely in achievements,
but in the spaces and works taht still speak,
and in the people he has inspired.

The hearts he has lit with fire—
a fire to speak our vernacular,
and create what is truly ours.

The Art Piece

John saw Demas Nwoko's story and saw a man emerging from a path rich with wisdom, rich with identity.

A man who knows not only where he is going, but remembers where he is coming from.

Mr Emmanuel crafted John's interpretation into this piece using Baba's soil clay

*

The Art Piece

This piece does not attempt to portray Demas Nwoko as a figure.

It portrays him as a condition.

A man shaped by soil.
And shaping with soil.

The relief emerges rather than stands apart suggesting that authorship, in vernacular practice, is never detached from place.

The surface is intentionally restrained.
No excess.
No spectacle.

Simply a silent revolution.

Conclusion

This project began as a gift.
But it quickly br=ecame a question.

What does it mean to design when vernacular is treated not a reference, but as spirit?

What happens when soil is not a backdrop, but a collaborator?

We realised that to tell this story truthfully, we could not speak about Demas Nwoko in the usual way.

Instead, we chose to speak through the ideas his work has given us.

The postcards.
The low-relief art piece.
This digital space.

Each is an interpretation
our way of translating philosophy into form.

This Is Not an Ending

To say vernacular is the natural langauge of design is not a romantic claim.
It is a practical one.

In a time when global design trends move faster than local realities,
Demas Nwoko's work reminds us that releveance comes from alignment, not speed.

If his work has been recognised beyind Nigeria,
it is because it never tried to leave home first.

This landing page is not a conclusion.
It is an opening.

An invitation to see vernacular
not as style, but as systems.
Not as memory, but as method.
Not as resistance, but as fluency.

The langauge was always there.
